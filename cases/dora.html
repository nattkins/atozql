The Problem

The Register of Information Is a Soviet Exercise

DORA's Register of Information is 15 templates organised into 7 functional groups. It maps every ICT third-party provider, every contract, every subcontractor, every business function dependent on external technology. It requires LEI validation, supply chain depth mapping, concentration risk identification, and cross-referencing across all templates.

Here's what actually happens: a compliance officer emails department heads asking them to fill in a spreadsheet. The department heads forward it to someone junior. That person guesses. The spreadsheet goes back up the chain. Nobody validates the LEIs. Nobody checks the subcontractor chains. Nobody maps the concentration risks. The Register passes initial submission because the format is correct — but the content is fiction.

This is The Soviet archetype in action. The centre demands the Register. The edge fills it in to survive the audit, not to tell the truth. The centre can't verify because they don't have visibility into the actual systems. Everyone knows the Register is incomplete, but the game rewards submission over accuracy.

The ZQL Approach

Federate the Register From Live Systems

Instead of asking humans to fill in spreadsheets, ZQL federates the Register of Information from the systems that already contain the data: contract management systems, CMDBs, vendor portals, risk registers, and procurement platforms.

How It Works

ZQL adapters connect (read-only) to your existing systems. The DORA lens maps each system's native data onto the 15-template structure. Where data is missing or inconsistent, ZQL surfaces the gap rather than hiding it — because a known gap is manageable, while an unknown gap is a liability.

The Register isn't generated once and filed. It's a live federation — every query pulls current data from source systems. When a contract changes, the Register reflects it. When a subcontractor relationship shifts, the concentration risk map updates. When an LEI lapses, the validation catches it.

The key insight: DORA doesn't just require a Register — it requires that the Register be accurate. Article 28(3) mandates that financial entities "identify and assess" ICT concentration risk. You can't assess what you can't see. Federation makes the invisible visible.

The Hidden Risk

Concentration Risk Is a Graph Problem

Your organisation uses Microsoft 365. Your payment processor uses Azure. Your CRM runs on Azure. Your data warehouse runs on Azure. Your third-party risk platform runs on Azure. You've reported five separate ICT arrangements — but you have one concentration risk: Microsoft.

Now go deeper. Microsoft's data centres are hosted by Equinix. Your backup provider also uses Equinix. Your disaster recovery site uses Equinix. Three apparently independent providers share a single fourth-party dependency that nobody mapped because nobody looked past the first tier.

ZQL computes concentration risk from the actual dependency graph — not from self-reported assessments. It traverses third-party and fourth-party relationships, identifies convergence points, and flags concentration risks that wouldn't be visible from any single system's perspective. This is a Q³ operation: the answer depends not just on the data (Q¹) or the context (Q²) but on the game being played between parties who may not want their dependencies known.

What the Regulator Will Ask

When the ESAs review your Register of Information, they won't just check format compliance. They'll ask:

1
Completeness: Have you documented ALL ICT third-party arrangements, including intra-group? Template B_02.01 must be exhaustive.
2
Depth: Have you mapped subcontractor chains? Template B_05.02 requires rank-in-chain for every provider relationship.
3
Concentration: Have you identified where multiple arrangements converge on single providers? Article 28(5) requires concentration risk assessment.
4
Currency: Is this Register current, or was it accurate six months ago? DORA requires ongoing maintenance, not annual snapshots.
5
Provenance: Can you show where each data point came from? If challenged, can you trace an answer to its source system?

A spreadsheet can answer question 1, badly. Only a federated system can answer questions 2–5.
